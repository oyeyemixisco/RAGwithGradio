The history of artificial intelligence begins in 1950 when Alan Turing published his landmark paper "Computing Machinery and Intelligence," introducing the famous Turing Test as a measure of machine intelligence. Turing proposed that if a machine could converse indistinguishably from a human, it could be considered intelligent.

In 1956, John McCarthy coined the term "artificial intelligence" at the Dartmouth Conference, which is widely regarded as the founding event of AI as a field. McCarthy, along with Marvin Minsky, Claude Shannon, and Nathaniel Rochester, brought together researchers who believed that every aspect of learning and intelligence could in principle be simulated by a machine.

The following decade saw rapid optimism. Early programs like the General Problem Solver and ELIZA, a chatbot that simulated a psychotherapist, demonstrated that machines could perform tasks previously thought to require human intelligence. Governments and institutions poured funding into AI research, expecting breakthroughs within a generation.

However, progress stalled in the 1970s. Computers lacked the processing power and memory to tackle real-world complexity. Funding dried up in what became known as the first AI Winter. A second wave of enthusiasm in the 1980s, driven by expert systems that encoded human knowledge into rule-based programs, also collapsed by the early 1990s when these systems proved too rigid and expensive to maintain.

The modern era of AI began with the rise of machine learning. Instead of programming rules by hand, researchers trained statistical models on large datasets. A turning point came in 2012 when a deep neural network called AlexNet won the ImageNet competition by a dramatic margin, demonstrating the power of deep learning for image recognition. The key ingredients were large datasets, graphics processing units for parallel computation, and new training techniques like dropout regularization.

The transformer architecture, introduced in the 2017 paper "Attention Is All You Need" by Vaswani and colleagues at Google, revolutionized natural language processing. Unlike recurrent neural networks that processed sequences word by word, transformers processed entire sequences in parallel using a mechanism called self-attention, which allowed the model to weigh the relevance of every word to every other word simultaneously. This made training dramatically faster and enabled models to capture long-range dependencies in text.

Transformers gave birth to the era of large language models. BERT, released by Google in 2018, demonstrated that pre-training on massive text corpora followed by fine-tuning on specific tasks achieved state-of-the-art results across nearly every language benchmark. GPT-3, released by OpenAI in 2020 with 175 billion parameters, showed that simply scaling up model size and training data produced remarkable emergent capabilities, including few-shot learning, code generation, and complex reasoning, without any task-specific training.

The release of ChatGPT in November 2022 marked a cultural turning point. The product reached one million users in five days and one hundred million users in two months, making it the fastest-growing consumer application in history. It demonstrated that conversational AI had crossed a threshold of usefulness that resonated with the general public, triggering a wave of investment and competition across the technology industry.

Today, AI research is advancing on multiple fronts simultaneously. Multimodal models process images, audio, and text together. Reasoning models chain multiple steps of thought before producing an answer. Agents use tools, browse the web, and execute code autonomously. The field that began with a single paper by Alan Turing has become one of the most consequential technological forces in human history.
