Space exploration is the investigation of outer space using both crewed and uncrewed spacecraft. It began in earnest during the Cold War, when competition between the United States and the Soviet Union drove rapid technological development. The Space Race produced some of the most dramatic achievements in human history within a single decade.

The Soviet Union launched the first artificial satellite, Sputnik 1, on October 4, 1957. The basketball-sized sphere transmitted radio signals for twenty-two days before its batteries died, but its psychological impact was enormous. It demonstrated that the Soviets had rockets powerful enough to reach orbit, and by implication, powerful enough to deliver nuclear warheads across continents. The United States responded by accelerating its own space program and founding NASA in 1958.

Yuri Gagarin became the first human to travel to space on April 12, 1961, completing one orbit of Earth in 108 minutes aboard Vostok 1. His flight was a triumph for Soviet prestige. Three weeks later, Alan Shepard became the first American in space, though his suborbital flight lasted only fifteen minutes. President John F. Kennedy responded to Soviet achievements by committing the United States to landing a man on the Moon before the end of the decade.

The Apollo program fulfilled Kennedy's promise. On July 20, 1969, Neil Armstrong and Buzz Aldrin landed the lunar module Eagle in the Sea of Tranquility while Michael Collins orbited above in the command module. Armstrong's words as he stepped onto the lunar surface became among the most famous in history. The program landed twelve astronauts on the Moon across six missions before concluding in 1972. Apollo remains the only crewed missions to land on another celestial body.

After Apollo, human spaceflight shifted focus to orbital space stations. The Soviet Mir station operated from 1986 to 2001, hosting 125 cosmonauts and conducting thousands of scientific experiments. The International Space Station, a joint project involving NASA, Roscosmos, the European Space Agency, JAXA, and the Canadian Space Agency, has been continuously inhabited since November 2000. It serves as a laboratory for research in biology, physics, astronomy, and human physiology in microgravity conditions.

Robotic exploration has extended humanity's reach far beyond where humans can travel. The Voyager probes, launched in 1977, conducted flybys of Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune before becoming the first human-made objects to enter interstellar space. The Mars rovers, from Sojourner in 1997 through Curiosity and Perseverance today, have transformed our understanding of Martian geology and the planet's potential for past microbial life. The James Webb Space Telescope, launched in December 2021, observes the universe in infrared wavelengths with unprecedented resolution, capturing light from galaxies formed just a few hundred million years after the Big Bang.

The twenty-first century has seen the rise of commercial spaceflight. SpaceX, founded by Elon Musk in 2002, developed reusable rocket technology that dramatically reduced launch costs. The Falcon 9 rocket can land its first stage booster vertically after launch, allowing it to be refurbished and reflown within days. SpaceX's Crew Dragon capsule has ferried NASA astronauts to the International Space Station since 2020, ending American dependence on Russian Soyuz spacecraft for crewed missions.

Artemis program aims to return humans to the Moon by the mid-2020s, this time with the goal of establishing a sustainable presence rather than brief visits. NASA plans to land the first woman and first person of color on the lunar surface. The program envisions a lunar Gateway space station in lunar orbit, serving as a staging point for surface missions and eventually for crewed missions to Mars. Mars itself remains the long-term destination for human exploration, with planners projecting crewed missions in the 2030s if funding and technology development remain on track.
