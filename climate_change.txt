Climate change refers to long-term shifts in global temperatures and weather patterns. While some climate variation is natural, scientific consensus holds that since the mid-twentieth century, human activities have been the dominant driver of observed warming. The primary mechanism is the emission of greenhouse gases, particularly carbon dioxide from burning fossil fuels, methane from agriculture and livestock, and nitrous oxide from industrial processes.

The greenhouse effect works as follows. Solar radiation passes through the atmosphere and warms the Earth's surface. The surface then emits infrared radiation back toward space. Greenhouse gases absorb this outgoing radiation and re-emit it in all directions, including back toward Earth. This traps heat in the lower atmosphere, raising global temperatures. Without any greenhouse effect the average surface temperature of Earth would be around minus eighteen degrees Celsius. The natural greenhouse effect keeps it at around fifteen degrees Celsius. The enhanced greenhouse effect from human emissions is pushing it higher still.

Since pre-industrial times, the average global temperature has risen by approximately 1.2 degrees Celsius. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change, established by the United Nations in 1988, has concluded in successive assessment reports that limiting warming to 1.5 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels requires halving global emissions by 2030 and reaching net zero by 2050. Beyond 1.5 degrees, the risks of irreversible damage to ecosystems, food systems, and human settlements increase substantially.

The observable effects of climate change are already widespread. Arctic sea ice extent is declining at roughly thirteen percent per decade. Glaciers on every continent are retreating. Sea levels are rising at an accelerating rate, currently about 3.7 millimeters per year, driven by thermal expansion of warming oceans and the melting of land ice. Extreme weather events including heatwaves, heavy rainfall, droughts, and intense tropical storms are becoming more frequent and severe.

Ocean acidification is a parallel consequence of rising carbon dioxide levels. About thirty percent of the carbon dioxide emitted by human activities is absorbed by the oceans, where it reacts with seawater to form carbonic acid, lowering ocean pH. Since the industrial revolution, ocean pH has dropped from 8.2 to 8.1, representing a twenty-six percent increase in acidity. This threatens coral reefs, shellfish, and marine ecosystems that form the base of ocean food chains.

The economic costs of inaction are projected to far exceed the costs of mitigation. The Stern Review on the Economics of Climate Change estimated that failing to act could reduce global GDP by five to twenty percent permanently, while stabilizing emissions would cost around one percent of global GDP per year. Renewable energy costs have fallen dramatically, with solar photovoltaic electricity now the cheapest source of new power generation in most of the world, creating economic opportunities alongside environmental necessity.

International responses include the Paris Agreement of 2015, signed by 196 parties, which commits countries to nationally determined contributions toward limiting warming. The agreement relies on voluntary pledges rather than binding targets, leading critics to argue it is insufficient given the pace of emissions growth. Some countries have additionally enacted carbon pricing mechanisms, clean energy mandates, and bans on internal combustion engine vehicles to drive structural transitions in their economies.

Adaptation strategies are increasingly necessary alongside mitigation. Coastal cities are investing in sea walls and flood defenses. Agricultural systems are shifting crop varieties and planting schedules to match changing rainfall patterns. Urban planners are redesigning cities to reduce heat island effects through green spaces, reflective surfaces, and improved ventilation. The challenge of climate change requires coordinated action across governments, industries, and individuals at a scale without precedent in human history.
